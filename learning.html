Quick Tips

Photography is all about the lighting. Always. Think about it... That's the only thing the imaging sensor captures: light. Be that with film or digital sensors, light is the only thing that ends up hitting the sensor. Now, when shooting with phone cameras, you have to pay even greater attention to lighting because of the current limitations they possess. If your scene isn't properly illuminated, your photos will express a lot of noise and reduced overall image quality. So always make sure you have strong enough light sources for what you're trying to capture. The Sun is usually a great one, and it's free!
With that in mind, it's also important to learn the Dynamic Range of your phone's camera and use it to your advantage. There is no such thing as "low dynamic range" in modern cameras: It's all about the ratios. If one side of the scene is too dark, you can try bouncing some of the key-light off to that side, or put more lights. It's all about the lighting—remember! If your shot isn't looking good, it's not the camera... The lighting is probably off.

There's also a lot you can do through the basic parameters in your camera: You can tweak the White Balance(Temperature and Tint) to change the mood or for effect;
Tweak the shutter speed to capture light trails, light paintings etc.; and more.

Experimenting with a flashlight—or other small light fixture—in a dark room is quite fun, I find. You could give it a try.

You might also want to do some of the many photography challenges out there, to expose yourself to a range of different ideas and to practice. Looking at other people's work to get inspired might also be a good idea, but there might also be value in isolating yourself from other people's work. That way you'll probably start expressing a style of your own, over time, but you shouldn't stop practicing and trying new things, otherwise you might end up doing the same things, over and over, and never evolve.
Apparently, there's an online mobile photography competition, so you'll probably find interesting phone photos there, for inspiration: mobilephotoawards.com.
Searching for "phone photography" will bring many interesting ones as well, for sure.

So go wild and have fun!

The usual basic photography tips also apply:

  • Use a tripod, to avoid unintentionally-shaky photos;
  • "Zoom with your feet": A fixed focal length camera shouldn't limit you. If you want a tighter framing, just walk closer to your subject. Or, get a set of mini lenses(for phones);
  • You might want to avoid shooting directly into light sources if it's not intentional/for effect because the light will spread all over your image and will ruin the contrast and clarity in you photo;
  • Make sure you have plenty of storage space available, or an easy way to transfer your photos, otherwise you'll run out of space very quickly;
  • Take the time to "perfect" the shot, to refine it! Snap a couple of photos, try different framing and angles, change things in the scene if necessary.
